##  ENGLISH SUMMARY (Simple)

This chapter explains why financial bubbles keep happening and why people fail to learn from them.

Bubbles are often blamed on greed, but the author argues that greed alone is an incomplete explanation. People usually make decisions that make sense to them at the time, based on limited information.

A major reason bubbles form is that **investors with different time horizons are playing different games**, yet they all influence the same prices.

There is no single ‚Äúrational‚Äù price for an asset. The right price depends on:

* how long you plan to hold it,
* what your goals are,
* and what game you‚Äôre playing.

A day trader can rationally buy an overpriced stock because they plan to sell it within hours. A long-term investor buying the same stock at the same price may be setting themselves up for disaster.

Bubbles form when:

* rising prices attract short-term traders,
* short-term momentum feeds on itself,
* and price-setting power shifts from long-term investors to short-term traders.

The damage occurs when long-term investors start copying short-term traders without realizing they are playing a different game.

The same logic applied to:

* the dot-com bubble,
* the housing bubble,
* stock flipping,
* and socially driven spending.

**Key lesson:**
Few things matter more with money than understanding your own time horizon and refusing to be influenced by people playing a different game than you.
