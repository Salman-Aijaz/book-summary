## üìå Quick Summary

### Key Takeaways (English)

* **Investor vs Speculator:**
  * Investor = Proper analysis + Safety of principal + Adequate return
  * Anything else = Speculation
  * Keep speculation separate from investments

* **Portfolio Strategy:**
  * Defensive investors: 50% bonds / 50% stocks (or 25%-75% range)
  * Rebalance when balance shifts by 5%
  * Stocks protect against inflation

* **Market Reality:**
  * Past high returns don't guarantee future returns
  * Bonds can also fall (1964-1970 example)
  * Future prices are never predictable

* **Investor Types:**
  * **Defensive:** Safety + peace of mind, large stable companies
  * **Aggressive:** Better returns, but only if can add ~5% extra return

* **Common Mistakes:**
  * Confusing speculation with investment
  * Buying "hot stocks" or new issues
  * Risking more than you can afford to lose

## 1. Investor vs Speculator

### üìù English Summary

#### 1.1 Definition of Investor

* An **investor** is someone who, after proper analysis, aims for:
  * **Safety of principal (capital protection)**
  * **Adequate return**
* Anything that does **not** meet these two conditions is called **speculation**.

#### 1.2 Confusion in Modern Usage

* Earlier (1930s‚Äì1940s), **stocks were considered risky and speculative**.
* Later, when stock prices went high and risky, people started calling **everyone an "investor"**, even reckless traders.
* This created **confusion between investing and speculation**.

#### 1.3 Danger of Mislabeling

* Calling speculators "investors" hides real risk.
* This can mislead people and cause heavy losses.
* Even stock exchanges themselves suffered losses by treating speculative stocks as investments.

#### 1.4 Speculation is Not Evil ‚Äî But Needs Limits

* Speculation is:
  * Not illegal
  * Not immoral
  * Sometimes necessary
* There is **intelligent speculation** and **unintelligent speculation**.

#### 1.5 Three Major Mistakes in Speculation

1. Thinking you are investing when you are actually speculating.
2. Serious speculation without enough knowledge or skill.
3. Risking more money than you can afford to lose.

#### 1.6 Margin & Hot Stocks

* Anyone buying on **margin** is automatically speculating.
* Buying "hot stocks" is speculation or gambling.
* Keep speculation **separate** from investments.

#### 1.7 Defensive Investor

* A defensive investor wants:
  * Safety
  * Peace of mind
* He must accept that:
  * Even good stocks now contain some speculative risk
  * The goal is to **keep that risk limited**

## 2. Portfolio Allocation Rules

### üìù English Summary

#### 2.1 Portfolio Allocation Rule

* Investors should split their money between:
  * **High-grade bonds**
  * **Leading common stocks**
* Bonds should be:
  * **Minimum 25%**
  * **Maximum 75%**
* Stocks will naturally be the opposite percentage.

#### 2.2 Simple & Practical Strategy

* Best and simplest approach:
  * **50% bonds / 50% stocks**
* Rebalance the portfolio if market changes disturb this balance by around **5%**.

#### 2.3 Flexible Strategy Based on Market Conditions

* Reduce stocks to **25%** if the market looks **dangerously high**.
* Increase stocks up to **75%** if prices fall and stocks become **attractive**.

#### 2.4 Returns in 1965

* High-grade taxable bonds gave around **4.5% return**.
* Tax-free bonds gave around **3.25%**.
* Stock dividends were only about **3.2%**, which suggested caution.

#### 2.5 Expected Long-Term Stock Return

* Normal expected stock return:
  * **3.5‚Äì4.5% dividends**
  * Plus **similar growth in value**
* Total expected stock return ‚âà **7.5% per year**.

#### 2.6 Combined Portfolio Return

* A 50‚Äì50 mix of stocks and bonds would yield about **6% annually before tax**.

#### 2.7 Inflation Protection

* Stock portion should help protect against:
  * **Loss of purchasing power due to inflation**.

#### 2.8 Warning About Past Performance

* Stock market growth from **1949‚Äì1964** averaged over **10%**, which was unusually high.
* Many people wrongly assumed:
  * Past high returns guaranteed future high returns.
* Authors warned:
  * High past growth may actually mean **stocks are now overpriced**.
  * Future returns could be **worse, not better**.

## 3. Market Changes Since 1964

### üìù English Summary

#### 3.1 Major Change After 1964

* The biggest change was a **sharp rise in interest rates**.
* High-grade corporate bonds now offered around **7.5% return**, compared to **4.5% in 1964**.
* Bond prices fell heavily, especially around **1970**, before partial recovery.

#### 3.2 Stock Dividends vs Bonds

* Dividend yield on DJIA-type stocks rose slightly during the 1969‚Äì70 market fall.
* Still, with Dow around **900**, dividend yield was **below 3.5%**, only slightly better than **3.2% in 1964**.
* Bonds became **much more attractive income-wise** than stocks.

#### 3.3 Heavy Losses in Long-Term Bonds

* Medium- to long-term bonds (around 20 years) suffered:
  * **Up to 38% decline in market price**.
* This was surprising because bonds were considered "safe."

#### 3.4 The Paradox

* Authors warned that **stocks might be overpriced**, but did NOT seriously consider:
  * That **high-grade bonds could also fall sharply**.
* Though they mentioned bond prices fluctuate with interest rates, the risk was **underestimated**.

#### 3.5 Stocks vs Bonds Performance

* If an investor bought DJIA stocks in 1964:
  * Small profit by 1971
  * Even at the 1970 bottom, losses were **less than long-term bonds**
* Long-term bonds actually did **worse than stocks** in price decline.

#### 3.6 Cash Equivalents Performed Best

* Investors holding:
  * U.S. savings bonds
  * Short-term corporate bonds
  * Savings accounts
* Had:
  * **No loss of principal**
  * **Higher income than stocks**
* Surprisingly, **cash did better than stocks**, despite inflation.

#### 3.7 Lesson on Predictability

* The future of security prices is **never predictable**.
* Bonds usually fluctuate less than stocks ‚Äî but **1964‚Äì1970 was a rare exception**.
* Money-market forces (usually ignored by individuals) caused major bond losses.

## 4. Expectations and Policy (1971-1972)

### üìù English Summary

#### 4.1 Market Situation in Late 1971

* Investors could earn:
  * **8% taxable interest** on good medium-term corporate bonds
  * **5.7% tax-free** on municipal/state bonds
  * **6%** on 5-year U.S. government bonds (with no market-value risk)
* DJIA stocks at around **900** yielded only **3.5% dividends**.

üëâ Bonds were clearly offering **higher and safer income** than stocks.

#### 4.2 Expected Returns on Stocks

* Defensive investor could expect:
  * **3.5% dividend yield**
  * Plus about **4% annual appreciation**
* Total **before-tax stock return ‚âà 7.5%**
* **After-tax stock return ‚âà 5.3%**
* This was roughly **equal to tax-free bond returns**.

#### 4.3 Stocks vs Bonds: 1971 Reality

* Compared to 1964:
  * Bond yields increased **much more** than stock yields.
* Bonds provide:
  * **More certainty**
  * **Better protection of principal**
* Conclusion:
  * In late 1971, **bonds appeared more attractive than stocks**.

#### 4.4 Why Not 100% Bonds?

Even though bonds looked better, certainty was impossible because:

* Inflation could unexpectedly rise
* Business profits could grow sharply
* Another speculative stock-market boom could occur

üëâ Any of these could make stocks outperform bonds.

#### 4.5 Core Policy (Still the Same)

* Defensive investors should:
  * **Always hold both bonds and stocks**
* Two acceptable strategies:
  * **50% bonds / 50% stocks**
  * Flexible range: **25%‚Äì75%** in either, based on judgment

üëâ Expected overall return stays similar regardless of mix.

#### 4.6 Expected Overall Return

* Combined portfolio return:
  * **~7.8% before tax**
  * **~5.5% after tax**
* This is:
  * Better than what conservative investors historically earned
  * Lower than the **14% stock returns after 1949**

#### 4.7 Why Past Returns Were Misleading

* From 1949‚Äì1969:
  * DJIA prices rose **5√ó**
  * Earnings/dividends only **2√ó**
* Most gains came from:
  * Changing investor psychology
  * Not real business growth
* Authors call this a **"bootstrap operation"**.

#### 4.8 Stock Selection for Defensive Investors

* Focus on:
  * Large, stable, financially strong companies
* Not limited only to Dow 30 stocks
* Beating the market consistently is **very unlikely**
* Even experts usually fail to outperform reliably.

#### 4.9 Warning Against "Hot" Stocks

* Buying:
  * New issues
  * Hot tips
  * Quick-profit recommendations
* Almost guarantees **below-average results** long-term.

#### 4.10 Additional Tools for Defensive Investors

1. **Investment funds / mutual funds**
2. **Professional investment counsel**
3. **Dollar-cost averaging**
   * Invest same amount regularly
   * Buy more when prices are low, less when high
4. **Formula investing**
   * Adjust stock/bond ratio opposite to market movements

## 5. Aggressive vs Defensive Investor

### üìù English Summary

#### 5.1 Aggressive Investor's Goal

* An aggressive (enterprising) investor wants **better-than-average returns**.
* First priority:
  * Make sure results are **not worse** than a defensive investor.
* Energy, intelligence, and effort **do not guarantee profits**.
* If used wrongly, these strengths can **cause losses**.

#### 5.2 Common Methods Used to Beat the Market

##### (a) Market Trading

* Buying stocks after prices rise.
* Selling after prices start falling.
* Includes **short selling** (selling borrowed shares to profit from price declines).
* Authors clearly reject trading as a sound investment method.

##### (b) Short-Term Selectivity

* Buying stocks with:
  * Rising earnings
  * Good expected near-term news
* Problem:
  * This information is usually **already priced in**.

##### (c) Long-Term Selectivity

* Buying companies with:
  * Strong past growth
  * Promising future potential (often tech, pharma, electronics)
* Risk:
  * Forecasting long-term success is **very difficult**.

#### 5.3 Why These Methods Usually Fail

* Two major obstacles:
  1. **Human error** (wrong predictions)
  2. **Professional competition** (Wall Street already knows)
* Even if predictions are correct:
  * Market prices often already reflect them.
* Beating professional analysts consistently is **highly unlikely**.

#### 5.4 Key Conclusion (Very Important)

To achieve above-average results, an investor must follow strategies that:

1. Are **logically sound**
2. Are **unpopular on Wall Street**

#### 5.5 Mispricing Exists ‚Äî But Is Hard to Exploit

* Stocks often become:
  * **Overvalued** due to popularity
  * **Undervalued** due to neglect or prejudice
* In theory:
  * Smart investors should profit from others' mistakes.
* In practice:
  * Undervalued stocks take **a long time** to rise.
  * Short selling overvalued stocks is:
    * Financially risky
    * Psychologically stressful
* Principle is correct, execution is **difficult**.

#### 5.6 Special Situations (Historically Profitable)

Examples include:

* Merger arbitrage
* Liquidations
* Workouts
* Certain hedged positions

**Past Results:**
* Historically delivered **~20% annual returns**
* With relatively low risk

**Why They Became Less Profitable:**
* Too much competition
* More failed mergers
* Returns declined over time

#### 5.7 Vanishing Opportunities (Key Insight)

* Many once-reliable strategies stopped working because:
  * Too many people started using them
* Examples:
  * Formula based on earnings & interest rates (worked before 1949)
  * Dow Theory (great before 1934, weak after)
  * Buying stocks below net working capital (very successful pre-1960s)

üëâ Markets **adapt and destroy easy profits**.

#### 5.8 Rare Opportunities Reappear Occasionally

* In 1970, market crash created:
  * Many stocks selling below working-capital value again
* Such opportunities:
  * Are rare
  * Appear mostly during **severe market declines**

#### 5.9 Final View on Aggressive Investing

* Even today, some stocks are:
  * Logically identifiable as **undervalued**
* Aggressive investing is worth the effort **only if**:
  * It can add **~5% extra return (before tax)** over market average
* Otherwise:
  * The extra work and risk are **not justified**
