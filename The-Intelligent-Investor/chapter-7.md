## **Summary in English**

In this section, Benjamin Graham defines the role of the enterprising investor as one who seeks returns above the market average through careful analysis and disciplined effort. Graham cautions that market timing‚Äîbuying low and selling high‚Äîappears simple in theory but is extremely difficult in practice. Successfully executing such strategies requires rare trading skill rather than ordinary intelligence, and most investors should not rely on it.

Graham critically examines the growth-stock approach and explains that strong past performance is usually already reflected in high stock prices. As a result, even when an investor correctly identifies a high-quality growth company, the returns may still be mediocre due to overvaluation. Historical evidence shows that even professional growth-oriented investment funds have struggled to consistently outperform the broader market, further weakening the case for growth investing as a superior strategy.

According to Graham, the best opportunities for the enterprising investor arise when the market makes emotional or analytical errors. He highlights three particularly promising areas: large but temporarily unpopular companies, securities selling at clear bargain prices relative to intrinsic value, and special situations such as mergers, reorganizations, or complex legal cases. These situations often lead to mispricing because the market tends to overreact to uncertainty and temporary difficulties.

The concept of bargain investing lies at the heart of Graham‚Äôs philosophy. A true bargain is not merely a low-priced stock but one whose underlying business value significantly exceeds its market price. Graham emphasizes that stable earnings, financial strength, and a margin of safety are essential. Secondary companies, which are not industry leaders, are frequently undervalued, but investors should only buy them at a meaningful discount to intrinsic value to achieve satisfactory long-term results.

A key lesson of the chapter is the importance of choosing a clear investment identity. An investor must decide whether to be defensive or fully enterprising; attempting to occupy a middle ground often leads to confusion and poor outcomes. Enterprising investing demands time, expertise, and emotional discipline, and is not suitable for everyone. For most investors, intelligence lies in patience, value consciousness, and a strict commitment to margin of safety rather than chasing popular ideas or market excitement.
