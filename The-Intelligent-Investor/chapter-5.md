### **Investment Merits of Common Stocks**

This chapter explains that although common stocks were once viewed as speculative, over the long term they have provided:

* Protection against inflation
* Higher average returns than bonds

However, these benefits disappear if stocks are purchased at **excessively high prices**. For the defensive investor, common stocks are necessary‚Äîeven if they are considered the ‚Äúlesser evil‚Äù compared to an all-bond portfolio.

---

### **Four Rules for the Defensive Investor**

1. **Adequate diversification**, but not excessive (10‚Äì30 stocks).
2. **Large, well-known, and conservatively financed companies.**
3. **Long record of continuous dividend payments** (at least since 1950).
4. **Reasonable price limits**

   * No more than 25√ó average earnings of past 7 years
   * No more than 20√ó last 12 months‚Äô earnings

---

### **Growth Stocks and Risk**

Growth stocks often sell at very high valuations, which introduces heavy speculation. Even top-quality growth stocks like IBM or Texas Instruments have suffered massive price declines.

Therefore, **growth stocks are not suitable for defensive investors**.

---

### **Portfolio Review**

Periodic review is sensible, but frequent changes are unnecessary if the portfolio was well selected initially. Any advisor should strictly follow the defensive rules.

---

### **Dollar-Cost Averaging**

Investing a fixed amount at regular intervals reduces timing risk. Historical evidence shows that dollar-cost averaging has produced profits over long periods regardless of market conditions.

### **True Meaning of Risk**

Temporary market price declines do not constitute real risk.

* Deterioration in the company‚Äôs fundamentals
* Paying too high a price relative to intrinsic value
